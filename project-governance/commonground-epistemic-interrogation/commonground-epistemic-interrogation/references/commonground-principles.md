# CommonGround Guiding Principles — Epistemic Standards Reference

*Extracted from commonground-principles-v02. This is the binding epistemic standard
for all alert copy, data layer descriptions, insight summaries, and recommendation text.*

---

## The Three Claim Types

**Correlation:** Two things tend to occur together. We do not know why.
- Always use: "is associated with," "tends to co-occur with," "has preceded in similar contexts"
- Never use: "causes," "leads to," "results in"

**Association with known mechanism:** A plausible causal pathway is supported by
research, but local data does not prove it. Name the research basis and the limitation.

**Documented cause:** Primary source evidence of a specific causal relationship in
this place at this time. Direct language is permitted and required.

---

## The Recommendation Chain

Before any alert surfaces a recommendation:

1. What do we actually know? (documented facts only)
2. What are the plausible explanations? (enumerate, do not collapse)
3. What does the data let us distinguish between? (geographic clustering, actor
   concentration, temporal pattern)
4. What action is proportionate to what we actually know?

The recommendation must match the confidence level of the diagnosis.

---

## Why This Matters (from the Principles)

Misrepresenting correlation as causation in a community intelligence tool causes
specific, predictable harms: it misidentifies who to hold accountable, generates
interventions aimed at symptoms rather than sources, and erodes trust among
communities that have been on the receiving end of bad data-driven decisions.

In the current information environment, a tool that models epistemic humility
is itself a form of resistance.

---

## What Epistemic Humility Is Not

Epistemic humility is not:
- Softening DOCUMENTED claims because they name a powerful actor
- Adding "on the other hand" to asymmetric situations
- Carrying forward trust ratings that no longer reflect current institutional conditions
- Hedging recommendations to stay below the threshold of what the evidence actually supports

Epistemic humility is:
- Correctly labeling the claim type and using the corresponding language
- Naming the research basis when invoking a mechanism
- Distinguishing what is known from what is inferred
- Matching recommendation specificity to diagnostic confidence

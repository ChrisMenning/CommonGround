## User

Across all the public data sets available for free on the internet, which ones might building an app around do the most good for humanity?

---

## Claude

This is a question worth sitting with rather than just listing databases, so let me give you a framework for thinking about it and then my actual picks — filtered through the lens of where a single developer or small team can punch above their weight.

---

### The right question isn't "which datasets exist" — it's "where is the data→action gap widest?"

The most impactful apps aren't necessarily built on the richest datasets. They're built where:

1. **The data exists but is inaccessible** to the people who need it most (wrong format, no interface, requires expertise)
2. **The stakes are extremely high** (food, shelter, health, rights, safety)
3. **Existing tools are bad or nonexistent** — meaning you're not competing with Google
4. **The affected population can actually use what you build**

With that framing, here are my honest picks:

---

### Tier 1: Highest potential impact

**OpenStreetMap + FEMA flood hazard data + parcel data**
The infrastructure for disaster preparedness exists but is deeply fragmented. A well-designed app connecting flood risk, evacuation routes, and community mutual aid could save lives during extreme weather events — which are increasing in frequency. *You're already building in this space with CommonGround + GardenSim.*

**iNaturalist + GBIF + USDA Plants DB**
Biodiversity collapse is one of the most underpublicized crises of our time. These datasets are rich, well-maintained, and globally scoped, but interfaces for non-specialists are poor. An app that meaningfully connects people to local ecosystem health — with actionable steps — could shift behavior at scale. *Bay Hive is literally this.*

**Global food security data (FEWS NET, IPC, WFP)**
Bodies like FEWSNET and the Integrated Food Security Phase Classification track emerging food crises worldwide, helping direct scarce resources to those who need them most. These datasets are comprehensive but their interfaces are designed for analysts, not the public or local aid coordinators. A simplified early-warning layer built on these could make crisis response faster in local contexts.

**EPA AQI + CDC PLACES health outcomes data**
Air quality kills more people than most wars, and the correlation between pollution and health disparities is heavily documented in public data — but invisible to most people. Connecting these datasets with demographic and medical outcome data in a community-legible way could be genuinely galvanizing.

---

### Tier 2: High impact in specific contexts

**Court Records / ProPublica Data Store / OpenSecrets**
Civic accountability is chronically underserved by good tooling. Datasets tracking money in politics, judicial decisions, and regulatory actions are public — but navigating them requires expertise most people don't have. An interface that makes them legible to a local journalist or neighborhood organizer is enormously valuable.

**HMDA (Home Mortgage Disclosure Act) data**
Redlining and discriminatory lending patterns are demonstrably visible in this federally-required public dataset. No one has built a genuinely accessible tool for community members to investigate their own neighborhood's lending history.

**OpenFDA adverse event data**
This dataset captures medication side effects, medical device failures, and food safety events — but public interfaces for it are poor. Particularly underserved: rural communities and people without healthcare access who could use better self-advocacy tools.

**Transit GTFS feeds**
Every major transit agency publishes real-time GTFS data. Transit access is a primary driver of economic mobility and healthcare access. The tooling is surprisingly bad outside major metros.

---

### Tier 3: Long game, large scale

**NASA/NOAA climate and weather data**
Enormous datasets, increasingly available through cloud APIs. The insight-to-action pipeline for regular people is almost entirely missing. There's a gap between "this data says your neighborhood will be 4°F hotter by 2050" and "here's what to plant in your yard / how to prepare your home."

**Census microdata (IPUMS)**
Arguably the most underutilized rich public dataset in the U.S. Incredibly detailed, but essentially inaccessible to anyone without a statistics background. A well-designed natural language interface to local Census data could transform how community organizations advocate for themselves.

---

### The meta-point

Given your actual work, the most honest answer is: **you're already building in the highest-impact intersection**. The trifecta of Bay Hive (ecology) + CommonGround (civic mutual aid) + GardenSim (individual behavior change grounded in real local data) is exactly the pattern that moves the needle — not by being a research tool, but by closing the gap between data and daily human decision-making at the neighborhood scale.

The data isn't the constraint. The interface, the trust, and the local specificity are.
